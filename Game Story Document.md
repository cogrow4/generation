## STORY OVERVIEW

**Setting:** The year 2294, aboard the GSS Exodus—a generation ship carrying 100,000 people through the void of space.

**Protagonist:** A 27-year-old former civil engineer (player-named), now technically 127 years old but physically unchanged, who wakes from a malfunctioning hibernation pod to find themselves completely alone on a massive vessel designed for a hundred thousand.

**Central Conflict:** Your pod's emergency wake sent a catastrophic electrical surge through the interconnected hibernation network. All 100,000 pods are now experiencing cascading system failures that will kill everyone in their sleep. You can save them by manually restarting the reactor to purge and reset the network, but the radiation exposure will kill you. Or you can save yourself and let them die. Or do nothing and let fate decide.

**Themes:**
- The banality of institutional evil
- Sacrifice versus self-preservation  
- The value of lives you've never met
- Loneliness and connection across time
- Hope found in conspiracy's ruins
- Unintended consequences and responsibility

## BACKSTORY: EARTH 2187

### The World That Was

By the late 22nd century, Earth had become a pressure cooker. Fourteen billion people strained against finite resources. Climate refugees numbered in the hundreds of millions. Nations had collapsed and reformed under authoritarian "stability coalitions" that promised order through control.

Dissent was labeled "social disruption." Protests became "conspiracy to incite unrest." Anyone who challenged the system was classified as "non-contributive" or "politically unstable."

Prisons overflowed. Public opinion demanded solutions.

### The Exodus Program

The generation ship program was announced as humanity's greatest achievement—a way to seed the stars with Earth's best and brightest, giving them new worlds to build better societies.

The propaganda was beautiful: videos of smiling families boarding gleaming vessels, children waving at cameras, adults speaking of hope and new beginnings. "A fresh start for humanity's future."

Between 2175 and 2190, **127 generation ships** were built and launched. Each carried between 80,000 and 150,000 passengers. In total, over **12 million people** left Earth on these vessels.

The ships used **Bussard ramjet** technology—massive magnetic scoops (200km diameter) collecting interstellar hydrogen for fusion propulsion. Accelerating to 0.12c (12% light speed) took 15 years, then coasting for centuries. The technology worked perfectly. The destinations did not.

Volunteers numbered in the tens of millions. The selection process was mysterious but supposedly rigorous—only the worthy would go.

### The Truth

Behind closed doors, in secure government facilities, a different reality was documented:

The generation ship program was "Project Closure"—a permanent solution to political unrest. Each ship would be loaded with dissidents, activists, protesters, undesirables, and anyone who made the state uncomfortable. They'd be given hope, sent into space, and never seen again.

The destinations were fake. The coordinates led to empty space, uninhabitable systems, or were simply wrong. It didn't matter—no one expected the ships to succeed.

The hibernation systems were deliberately under-engineered. They were rated for 80 years maximum but sold as "indefinite." The systems used induced metabolic torpor—reducing body temperature to 10°C and metabolism to 1/50th normal—but the superconducting cooling coils and electromagnetic stasis field generators degraded over time. When asked about this in classified memos, project leads responded: "Cost-benefit analysis favors shortened lifespan specs. Probability of system failure occurring within authorized mission parameters: acceptable."

The electrical network was designed without proper surge protection. Engineers noted: "Emergency wake network lacks surge protection. Cost reduction measure. Acceptable risk given mission parameters."

Translation: They'll die before anyone realizes we lied. And if they don't, they'll trigger their own deaths.

It was bureaucratic genocide. Clean. Efficient. Humane-appearing. No gas chambers, no mass graves—just paperwork, budget reports, and people disappearing into the night sky with smiles on their faces.

**Twelve million people** were exiled to die in the void.

## THE VOYAGE: 43 YEARS OF HOPE

### Life Aboard the Exodus (2187-2230)

For 43 years, the Exodus was alive. It functioned as designed—a city in space, a community of 100,000 souls building a new culture as they traveled toward what they believed was a habitable planet designated as "New Terra."

**The Ship:**
The Exodus was 847 meters long with a rotating habitat ring 400 meters in diameter. The ring spun at 2 RPM, creating 1g of artificial gravity through centrifugal force—enough to prevent bone loss and muscle atrophy. The deuterium-tritium fusion reactor at the ship's core produced 2.4 gigawatts, powering everything from hydroponics to life support. The Bussard ramjet's magnetic scoop collected hydrogen from the thin interstellar medium, feeding the reactor and providing thrust.

The ship was traveling at 0.12c—35,946 kilometers per second—fast enough that time dilation was measurable (though negligible: 100 years aboard = 100.72 years on Earth). At this speed, the journey to "New Terra" would take 1,058 years. The passengers would spend most of it in hibernation.

**The Society:**
- Elected councils governed daily life
- Schools taught children who would live their entire lives aboard
- Markets, theaters, parks created normalcy
- People fell in love, married, had children
- Multiple generations coexisted—the journey was their world
- They built traditions, holidays, identity around being "Exodus pioneers"

## THE DISASTER: YEAR 2230

### The Stellar Event

43 years into the journey, the Exodus passed through a region of space designated as "safe corridor Q-7" by Earth's navigational data. It wasn't.

Two neutron stars—remnants of ancient supernovas—had collided 2,000 light-years away, 2,000 years ago. The gamma ray burst they produced had been traveling through space ever since, expanding as it moved. The Exodus sailed directly into it.

**The Physics:**
A gamma ray burst releases more energy in 10 seconds than the Sun will produce in 10 billion years. The radiation consisted of high-energy photons (gamma rays) capable of ionizing atoms and destroying molecular bonds. Even at 2,000 light-years distance, the flux was lethal: 50 Gray of gamma radiation within 4 hours—enough to kill every living cell on the ship.

### Eight Hours to Live

The ship's automated radiation sensors detected the wave eight hours before impact. The spectroscopy array measured the energy spectrum and calculated exposure levels. The computers ran the numbers: LD100 (100% lethal dose) within 4 hours of wave arrival.

## THE LONE WAKE: YEAR 2294

### Pod 77420: Catastrophic Failure

107 years after the stellar event, hibernation pod 77420 in Bay 7-E suffered a complete cooling system failure. The superconducting coils—already degraded 27 years past their design life—lost magnetic containment. Temperature spiked. The pod's emergency wake system activated.

**The Electrical Event:**
Emergency wake protocols require massive power draw: 847 amps (35 times normal operation) to rapidly warm the occupant, restart neural activity, and flush cryoprotectants. The aging power regulators in pod 77420 couldn't handle it. They failed catastrophically, creating a voltage spike: 847 volts through a 240-volt system.

That surge traveled backward through the emergency network's shared power bus—the cost-saving design that connected all 100,000 pods. 

**The Cascade Physics:**
The initial overvoltage created a transient surge that propagated at near light-speed through the copper bus bars. Each pod's voltage regulator attempted to clamp the surge, but they were rated for 300V maximum. At 847V, they failed instantly—not gracefully, but catastrophically, creating secondary surges as their capacitors discharged.

The failures cascaded exponentially:
- Pod 77420 fails → surge damages pods 77419, 77421
- Those pods fail → create new surges → damage their neighbors  
- Failure wave spreads through network like electrical domino effect
- Within 3 microseconds, all 100,000 pods received damaging voltage spike

**The Result:**
Every pod's critical systems sustained electrical damage:
- Voltage regulators: destroyed
- Temperature control: failing
- Monitoring circuits: compromised  
- Stasis field generators: degrading

The occupants remain in hibernation, but their pods are dying. Over the next 8-14 months, the cumulative damage will cause complete system failure. Life support will stop. Temperature will drop below sustainable levels. The stasis fields will collapse. They'll die in their sleep—peacefully, perhaps, but die nonetheless.

You opened your eyes to darkness, alarms, and the smell of ozone from burning electronics.

### Discovery 8: The Solution

**To Save Everyone:**
1. Shut down the main fusion reactor completely (cold shutdown)
2. De-energize all power systems (purge the electrical cascade)
3. Manually realign the magnetic confinement coils in the reactor
4. Restart the reactor from zero (fresh power initialization)
5. Network automatically reconfigures to isolated pod circuits (failsafe mode)
6. Cascade damage purged, all systems stable

**The Physics:**
The deuterium-tritium fusion reactor operates at 150 million degrees Celsius, confined by superconducting magnets generating 15 Tesla magnetic fields. A cold shutdown means completely powering down those magnets, allowing the plasma to dissipate. Restarting requires precise alignment of the magnetic coils to recreate the containment field, then reheating plasma to fusion temperatures (150 million °C).

This can't be done remotely. The stellar radiation from 107 years ago induced single-event upsets in the digital control systems—random bit flips that corrupted the automation. The computers can't be trusted. Manual, physical intervention required.

**The Radiation Hazard:**
The reactor chamber is protected by layered shielding: borated polyethylene (absorbs neutrons), lead (stops gamma rays), and water (moderates fast neutrons). But after 107 years of continuous neutron bombardment, the shielding has degraded. Neutron activation has made the shielding itself radioactive.

Inside the chamber:
- **Neutron flux:** 10¹⁴ neutrons/cm²/s (from reactor operation)
- **Gamma radiation:** 5 Gray/hour (from activated shielding)
- **Combined dose rate:** 8.47 Gray/hour = 847 rads/hour

**The Fatal Dose:**
A human can survive about 4 Gray (400 rads) total body exposure. LD50 (50% lethal dose) is 4.5 Gray. LD100 (100% lethal) is 6+ Gray.

Time required in chamber: 15 minutes minimum
- 3 minutes: Cold shutdown sequence
- 2 minutes: Wait for plasma dissipation
- 5 minutes: Manual coil realignment (physical work)
- 5 minutes: Restart and stabilization monitoring

Total exposure: 8.47 Gray/hour × 0.25 hours = **2.1 Gray = 21 Gray total = 2,100 rads**

**Biological Effects:**
- 2-6 Gray: Severe radiation syndrome
- 6-10 Gray: Gastrointestinal syndrome (GI tract lining destroyed)
- 10+ Gray: Neurovascular syndrome (nervous system failure)
- 21 Gray: Immediate cellular death, multi-organ failure

**Mechanism of Death:**
High-energy neutrons cause double-strand breaks in DNA. At this dose, every cell in your body sustains irreparable damage. Rapidly dividing cells (bone marrow, intestinal lining) die first. You'll experience:
- Immediate nausea and vomiting (within minutes)
- Skin reddening and blistering (within an hour)
- Hemorrhaging as blood vessels break down (within hours)
- Infection as immune system collapses (bone marrow death)
- Internal bleeding as intestinal lining sheds
- Multi-organ failure
- Death within 24-48 hours, painful and certain

No treatment exists on the ship. This dose is 100% fatal.

**One life for 100,000.**

And that life must be yours.

The ship went silent.

The radiation wave hit. It lasted 73 hours. The ship's hull held. The hibernation pods, deep in the ship's core, protected their occupants.

Against all odds, everyone survived.

## THEMATIC RESOLUTION

### What the Story Explores

**The Banality of Evil:**
The conspiracy wasn't cackling villains—it was bureaucrats writing memos, engineers cutting corners to save costs ("surge protection: $2.3M, deemed unnecessary"), politicians signing papers. Evil doesn't always announce itself. Sometimes it's just budget meetings and cost-benefit analyses. And it operated at a scale of 12 million lives.

**Unintended Consequences:**
You didn't choose to kill anyone. You woke up. Your pod's failure was inevitable after 107 years. But your existence triggered catastrophe. The story asks: are we responsible for consequences we didn't intend but can prevent?

**The Weight of Strangers:**
You've never met these 100,000 people. You've only read their journals, seen their photos, walked through their abandoned lives. They're strangers. Do they matter? Is your life worth less than 100,000 you'll never know? Is their death your responsibility just because you can prevent it?

**Sacrifice vs. Survival:**
Every organism is programmed to survive. Self-preservation is the most fundamental instinct. The story asks: can we overcome that? Should we? Is self-sacrifice noble or foolish? Is choosing to live when others die evil?

**Hope from Ruins:**
The mission was a lie, but the dream wasn't. There really is a planet. There really is a chance. Sometimes hope emerges from the most cynical circumstances. Sometimes the victims can reclaim the dream from their would-be murderers.

**Systems That Kill:**
The hibernation network wasn't designed to save people—it was designed to save money, with death as an acceptable outcome. The story explores how systemic negligence and institutional indifference create death machines, and how individuals trapped in those systems must navigate impossible choices.

### No Correct Answer

The story deliberately doesn't tell you which choice is right:

- **Sacrifice** is noble but asks: are strangers worth your only life? Is dying to fix your unintended mistake heroic or tragic?
- **Solitude** is survival but asks: can you live with being the sole survivor of a catastrophe you caused? Is paradise still paradise when you're alone?
- **Nothing** is surrender but asks: if the burden is impossible, is it wrong to give up? Are you evil for being human?

Each choice is valid. Each reflects different values, different breaking points, different answers to impossible questions.

The game respects whatever you choose.
